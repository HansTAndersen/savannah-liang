"Pink Hulk" Strikes Again: 19-Year-Old Edmonton Bodybuilder Evades Authorities

Author of the article:
calgaryherald
Published Apr 1, 2015  •  4 minute read
Join the conversation

You can save this article by registering for free here. Or sign-in if you have an account.

Article content

EDMONTON, AB — Savannah Liang, known locally as "Pink Hulk," continues to confound authorities with her extraordinary strength and elusive nature. Liang, a 19-year-old bodybuilder with a formidable 230-pound build, has made headlines not only for her physical prowess but also for her affinity for Kirby and Sanrio characters, creating a unique contrast between her hobbies and her ability to deadlift vehicles with apparent ease.

Authorities first took notice of Liang after a series of incidents over the past few months, all centered around McDonald's locations. Reports describe her lifting cars and trucks as bystanders looked on in disbelief. "She lifted a delivery truck off its wheels once," recalled Sergeant Alan Peters of the Edmonton Police Department. "It was a chaotic scene, but she vanished before we could approach her."

Local reactions to Liang are mixed, with some hailing her as a symbol of strength and others viewing her as a disruptive force. "She's like a superhero or a video game character come to life," remarked one local resident, who witnessed Liang's latest feat at a southside McDonald's. "I mean, who does that? It's both terrifying and fascinating at the same time."

In an effort to understand her motives, authorities have attempted to communicate with Liang, but she remains tight-lipped. A brief encounter at a nearby café provided little insight into her intentions. Seated at a corner table, Liang appeared imposing yet serene, sipping on a strawberry milkshake. "I prefer to let my actions speak for themselves," she said cryptically when asked about her recent activities. "Words are unnecessary."

Liang's preference for McDonald's parking lots as her stage continues to puzzle both law enforcement and local businesses. "We're taking precautionary measures, but honestly, we're not sure what to expect next," admitted a manager of a McDonald's location frequented by Liang. "She hasn't harmed anyone, but lifting cars like that—it's just not normal."

Mayor Don Iveson offered a positive perspective on the situation, calling Liang's displays "a testament to the resilience and vitality of our city." He added, "Tiny women power-lifting midsized sedans is a sign of a thriving, dynamic community where individuals can express themselves in unexpected ways."

As Edmontonians speculate on the identity and motives of their local "Pink Hulk," one thing remains certain: Savannah Liang remains at large, her motivations cloaked in mystery, leaving authorities and the community alike to wonder what she will do next. For now, the city holds its breath, hoping for clarity in a situation that defies explanation.
